### **Question No. 6**
Although compilers are designed to translate a particular language, they
often allow calls to subprograms that are coded in some other language
(typically, Fortran, C, or assembler). Why are such “foreign calls” al-
lowed? In what ways do they complicate compilation?

### **Solution**
Foreign calls are allowed because different programming languages are often better suited for different tasks. For example, assembly language may be used for highly optimized hardware-specific routines, C may be used for operating-system interfaces or performance-critical code, and Fortran is commonly used for scientific and numerical computing. Allowing foreign calls enables programmers to reuse existing libraries, take advantage of specialized code, improve performance, and integrate software written in multiple languages rather than rewriting everything in a single language.

However, foreign calls complicate compilation in several ways. Different languages may use different calling conventions, parameter-passing mechanisms, memory layouts, naming schemes, runtime systems, and data representations. For example, one language may pass arguments by value while another passes them by reference, or one language may represent strings and arrays differently from another. Exception handling, stack management, and garbage collection may also be incompatible across languages. As a result, the compiler and linker must generate special interface code, often called “glue code” or “wrappers,” to translate between the conventions of the different languages. This increases compiler complexity and can also make debugging and optimization more difficult because the compiler has incomplete knowledge about externally written routines.
