### Exercise 8

Consider the following two grammars.

#### Grammar (a)

```txt
Start → E $

E → ( E plus E )
  | num
```

This grammar says that an expression `E` is either:

```txt
num
```

or a fully parenthesized expression of the form:

```txt
( E plus E )
```

Examples generated by this grammar are:

```txt
num
(num plus num)
((num plus num) plus num)
(num plus (num plus num))
```

This grammar is **not ambiguous** because the parentheses clearly determine the structure of every expression. There is no choice about how to group the operands.

---

#### Grammar (b)

```txt
Start → E $

E → E plus E
  | num
```

This grammar says that an expression `E` is either:

```txt
num
```

or:

```txt
E plus E
```

This grammar is **ambiguous** because an input such as:

```txt
num plus num plus num $
```

can be grouped in two different ways:

```txt
(num plus num) plus num
```

or:

```txt
num plus (num plus num)
```

---

### Two Distinct Derivations for Grammar (b)

#### Derivation 1: Left Grouping

```txt
Start
⇒ E $
⇒ E plus E $
⇒ E plus E plus E $
⇒ num plus E plus E $
⇒ num plus num plus E $
⇒ num plus num plus num $
```

This corresponds to:

```txt
(num plus num) plus num
```

---

#### Derivation 2: Right Grouping

```txt
Start
⇒ E $
⇒ E plus E $
⇒ num plus E $
⇒ num plus E plus E $
⇒ num plus num plus E $
⇒ num plus num plus num $
```

This corresponds to:

```txt
num plus (num plus num)
```

---

### Final Answer

```txt
Grammar (a) is not ambiguous.
Grammar (b) is ambiguous.
```

Grammar (b) is ambiguous because the same input string:

```txt
num plus num plus num $
```

has two different parse structures.
